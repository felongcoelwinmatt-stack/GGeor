Radiation Demystified

Radiation is energy that travels through space as waves or particles. It's all around us - from the sun's rays to the bananas we eat! But not all radiation is the same, and understanding the differences is key to using it safely and effectively.

Did You Know?

You receive about 2-3 mSv (millisieverts) of radiation every year just from natural sources like rocks, soil, and cosmic rays. That's equivalent to about 10 chest X-rays!

How Radiation Works

NUCLEUS

Unstable atoms release energy to become stable - this is radiation!

Types of Radiation & Their Effects

Different types of radiation have different properties and effects on living tissue. Here's what you need to know:

α

Alpha Radiation

Heavy, slow particles (2 protons + 2 neutrons). Stopped by paper or skin. Dangerous if inhaled or ingested.

β

Beta Radiation

Fast electrons. Penetrates skin but stopped by aluminum. Can cause skin burns.

γ

Gamma Radiation

High-energy photons. Very penetrating! Requires thick lead or concrete to stop. Used in cancer treatment.

n

Neutron Radiation

Released during nuclear fission. Highly penetrating and dangerous. Requires water or concrete shielding.

Biological Effects of Radiation

How Radiation Affects Cells

Radiation can damage DNA in cells. Low doses may be repaired, but high doses can cause cell death or mutations that lead to cancer.

Measuring Radiation

Sievert (Sv): Measures biological effect
Gray (Gy): Measures absorbed energy
Becquerel (Bq): Measures radioactivity

Dose Matters!

Effects depend on dose:
• <0.1 Sv: No immediate effects
• 0.5-1 Sv: Nausea, temporary blood changes
• >6 Sv: Usually fatal without treatment

Natural vs. Man-Made Radiation

About 85% of human radiation exposure comes from natural sources (radon gas, cosmic rays, food). Only 15% comes from medical procedures and other man-made sources!

Real-World Applications

Medicine

X-rays, CT scans, PET scans, and radiation therapy for cancer treatment save millions of lives each year.

Agriculture

Radiation is used to create disease-resistant crops and to sterilize pests in the Sterile Insect Technique.

Industry

Used for sterilizing medical equipment, inspecting welds, measuring material thickness, and smoke detectors.

Energy

Nuclear power plants generate about 10% of the world's electricity with zero carbon emissions!

Radiation Safety & Protection

The key principles of radiation protection are summarized by the acronym TIME, DISTANCE, SHIELDING:

Minimize TIME

Reduce the time spent near radiation sources to lower your total exposure.

Maximize DISTANCE

Radiation intensity decreases with the square of the distance. Doubling your distance reduces exposure to 1/4!

Use SHIELDING

Use appropriate materials between you and the source: lead for gamma, plastic for beta, etc.
